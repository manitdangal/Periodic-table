Curium

Curium, symbol Cm, is a synthetic radioactive element with atomic number 96. It was discovered in 1944 and named in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie for their pioneering research on radioactivity.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Cm
  • Atomic Number: 96
  • Atomic Mass: 247.07
  • Density: 13.51 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1,400 °C (estimated)
  • Boiling Point: Unknown (highly reactive and radioactive)

Importance in the Universe

Curium is important for its role in nuclear physics and applications involving radioactive isotopes. It is also studied for its potential use in nuclear reactors and as a source of energy in space exploration.

Key Applications

  • Scientific Research: Curium isotopes are used in scientific research, particularly in nuclear physics and the study of fission processes.
  • Space Exploration: Curium-242 is used in thermoelectric power sources for deep space missions.
  • Medical: Curium isotopes have been studied for use in cancer treatment and for the production of medical radioisotopes.

Did You Know?

Curium was one of the first elements to be created synthetically and was named in honor of the famous scientist Marie Curie, who was a pioneer in the study of radioactivity.

Isotopes of Curium

The most stable isotope, Curium-247, has a half-life of 15.6 million years. It is used in various applications, including space exploration and scientific research on nuclear reactions.

Chemical Properties

Curium is highly radioactive and forms a variety of compounds, especially in the +3 oxidation state. It reacts with oxygen and water to form curium oxide and curium hydroxide.
