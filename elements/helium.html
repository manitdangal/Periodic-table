Helium

Helium, symbol He, is the second element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 2. It is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless noble gas, and it is the second lightest element in the universe, after hydrogen.

General Properties

  • Symbol: He
  • Atomic Number: 2
  • Atomic Mass: 4.002602 amu
  • Density: 0.1786 g/L (at 0°C and 1 atm)
  • State at Room Temperature: Gas
  • Melting Point: -272.2°C
  • Boiling Point: -268.93°C

Importance in the Universe

Helium is the second most abundant element in the universe, making up approximately 24% of its elemental mass. It is primarily formed through nuclear fusion in stars and during the Big Bang nucleosynthesis.

Key Applications

Helium is widely used in various industries and research fields:

  • Cryogenics: Helium is used in supercooling applications, including cooling superconducting magnets in MRI machines.
  • Aeronautics: Helium is used to fill airships and weather balloons because it is lighter than air and non-flammable.
  • Welding: Helium serves as a shielding gas in arc welding to prevent oxidation.
  • Deep-Sea Diving: Mixed with oxygen, helium is used in breathing gas to prevent nitrogen narcosis.

Did You Know?

Helium was first detected in the sun’s spectrum during a solar eclipse in 1868 before it was found on Earth. Its name is derived from the Greek word *helios*, meaning "sun."

Chemical Properties

Helium is chemically inert due to its complete outer electron shell, making it part of the noble gas group. Its inertness and low boiling point contribute to its unique applications.

Isotopes of Helium

Helium has two stable isotopes:

  • Helium-3 (³He): A rare isotope used in nuclear research and cryogenics.
  • Helium-4 (⁴He): The most common isotope, making up almost all naturally occurring helium.
