Titanium

Titanium, symbol Ti, is the 22nd element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 22. It is a transition metal known for its strength, low density, and corrosion resistance. Titanium is widely used in aerospace, medical devices, and high-performance applications due to these qualities.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Ti
  • Atomic Number: 22
  • Atomic Mass: 47.867 amu
  • Density: 4.506 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1668°C
  • Boiling Point: 3287°C

Importance in the Universe

Titanium is one of the most abundant elements in the Earth's crust and plays a role in the formation of stars. Its high stability and low density make it a critical element in many geological processes.

Key Applications

Titanium has a wide range of applications in various industries:

  • Aerospace Industry: Titanium is used in the construction of aircraft and spacecraft due to its strength and resistance to corrosion at high altitudes.
  • Medical Implants: Titanium's biocompatibility makes it ideal for use in medical implants like joint replacements and dental implants.
  • Military: Titanium is used in military applications, including armor plating and components for military aircraft.
  • Jewelry: Titanium is used to make durable and lightweight jewelry, often due to its hypoallergenic properties.

Did You Know?

Titanium is named after the Titans of Greek mythology due to its strength and durability. It is often referred to as a "space-age metal" because of its applications in aerospace technology.

Isotopes of Titanium

Titanium has five naturally occurring isotopes, with Titanium-48 being the most abundant. The isotopes of titanium are stable and do not undergo significant radioactive decay.

Chemical Properties

Titanium is highly reactive and forms compounds with oxygen, chlorine, and other elements:

  • Titanium Dioxide (TiO₂): A white pigment used in paints, sunscreens, and cosmetics.
  • Titanium Tetrachloride (TiCl₄): Used in the production of titanium metal and as a precursor for other titanium compounds.
  • Titanium Alloys: Titanium is often alloyed with aluminum, vanadium, and other metals to enhance its properties for use in various applications.
