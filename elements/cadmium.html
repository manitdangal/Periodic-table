Cadmium

Cadmium, symbol Cd, is the 48th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 48. It is a soft, bluish-white metal that is primarily used in batteries and coatings but is toxic in large amounts.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Cd
  • Atomic Number: 48
  • Atomic Mass: 112.41 amu
  • Density: 8.65 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 321.1°C
  • Boiling Point: 765°C

Importance in the Universe

Cadmium is not abundant in the Earth's crust but is essential in some biological processes. Its compounds are also used in industry despite its toxicity.

Key Applications

Cadmium is used in various industries, primarily:

  • Batteries: Cadmium is a key component of rechargeable nickel-cadmium (Ni-Cd) batteries.
  • Coatings: Cadmium plating is used for corrosion resistance on steel parts.
  • Semiconductors: Cadmium compounds are used in the production of semiconductors for electronics.

Did You Know?

Cadmium was first isolated in 1817 by Friedrich Stromeyer, and its name comes from the Greek word for "calamine," which was the mineral from which it was first extracted.

Isotopes of Cadmium

Cadmium has several isotopes, with Cadmium-113 being the most commonly used in scientific research.

Chemical Properties

Cadmium forms compounds such as:

  • Cadmium Sulfide (CdS): Used in pigments and as a semiconductor.
  • Cadmium Chloride (CdCl₂): A compound used in electroplating and as a catalyst in chemical reactions.
