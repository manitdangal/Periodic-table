Uranium

Uranium, symbol U, is a radioactive actinide metal with atomic number 92. It is the heaviest naturally occurring element and plays a key role in nuclear energy production.

General Properties

  • Symbol: U
  • Atomic Number: 92
  • Atomic Mass: 238.03
  • Density: 18.95 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1,132 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4131 °C (estimated)

Importance in the Universe

Uranium is the most well-known element used as fuel in nuclear reactors, and its isotopes are used for both energy production and in nuclear weapons. It is also found naturally in trace amounts in rocks, soil, and water.

Key Applications

  • Nuclear Power: Uranium-235 is the primary isotope used in nuclear reactors to generate electricity through nuclear fission.
  • Nuclear Weapons: Uranium-235 is used in the production of atomic bombs due to its ability to undergo rapid fission.
  • Medical: Uranium compounds are used in the treatment of cancer and in diagnostic imaging for its radioactivity.

Did You Know?

Uranium was discovered in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth, and it was named after the planet Uranus, which had been discovered just a few years earlier in 1781.

Isotopes of Uranium

The most important isotopes of uranium are Uranium-238 and Uranium-235. Uranium-238 is the most abundant isotope found in nature, while Uranium-235 is fissile and used in nuclear reactors and weapons.

Chemical Properties

Uranium is a heavy, dense metal that is highly reactive. It readily forms compounds such as Uranium oxide (UO₂) and Uranium hexafluoride (UF₆), which are key to its use in the nuclear industry.
