Bromine

Bromine, symbol Br, is the 35th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 35. It is a reddish-brown liquid at room temperature and has a strong, unpleasant odor.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Br
  • Atomic Number: 35
  • Atomic Mass: 79.904 amu
  • Density: 3.1028 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Liquid
  • Melting Point: -7.2°C
  • Boiling Point: 58.8°C

Importance in the Universe

Bromine is found in trace amounts in the Earth's oceans and is crucial in various chemical processes in nature. It plays an important role in chemical industries and environmental chemistry.

Key Applications

Bromine is used in several applications:

  • Flame Retardants: Bromine compounds are commonly used as fire retardants in materials such as plastics.
  • Pesticides: Bromine is used in some pesticide formulations.
  • Water Treatment: Bromine is used in swimming pool water treatment as an alternative to chlorine.

Did You Know?

Bromine was discovered in 1826 by the French chemist Antoine Jérôme Balard. It was named after the Greek word "bromos," meaning stench, due to its strong odor.

Isotopes of Bromine

Bromine has two stable isotopes, Bromine-79 and Bromine-81, both of which are used in scientific and industrial applications.

Chemical Properties

Bromine is highly reactive and forms various compounds such as:

  • Hydrobromic Acid (HBr): A strong acid used in organic synthesis and as a disinfectant.
  • Bromine Gas (Br₂): A toxic, reddish-brown gas used in the production of other chemicals.
