Zinc

Zinc, symbol Zn, is the 30th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 30. It is a bluish-white metal known for its corrosion resistance and is commonly used in galvanization and in alloys such as brass.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Zn
  • Atomic Number: 30
  • Atomic Mass: 65.38 amu
  • Density: 7.14 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 419.58°C
  • Boiling Point: 907°C

Importance in the Universe

Zinc is found in asteroids and is an essential element in biological systems. It plays a vital role in the functioning of enzymes and is involved in various metabolic processes in living organisms.

Key Applications

Zinc has various applications, including:

  • Galvanization: Zinc is primarily used to coat iron and steel to prevent rusting in a process called galvanization.
  • Alloys: Zinc is used to make alloys such as brass, which is used in musical instruments and decorative items.
  • Batteries: Zinc-carbon and alkaline batteries use zinc as an anode material.
  • Medicine: Zinc is an essential trace element in the human diet and is used in some medications and supplements.

Did You Know?

Zinc was first used in alloy form around 500 BCE, and it has been known since ancient times for its use in creating brass. It was first isolated as a pure metal in 1746 by Andreas Sigismund Marggraf.

Isotopes of Zinc

Zinc has several stable isotopes, with Zinc-64 being the most abundant. Zinc-67 is also important for nuclear applications and as a tracer in medical research.

Chemical Properties

Zinc is reactive with acids and alkalis. It forms compounds like:

  • Zinc Oxide (ZnO): Used in cosmetics, paints, and as a semiconductor in electronics.
  • Zinc Sulfide (ZnS): Used in luminescent paints and as a phosphor in electronics.
  • Zinc Chloride (ZnCl₂): Used in soldering and as a flux in galvanizing.
