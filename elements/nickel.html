Nickel

Nickel, symbol Ni, is the 28th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 28. It is a silvery-white metal, known for its strength, corrosion resistance, and excellent conductivity. Nickel is commonly used in alloys and batteries.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Ni
  • Atomic Number: 28
  • Atomic Mass: 58.693 amu
  • Density: 8.908 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1455°C
  • Boiling Point: 2913°C

Importance in the Universe

Nickel is found in meteorites and is an essential component of the Earth's core. It is also abundant in many astronomical objects, including stars and asteroids.

Key Applications

Nickel is widely used in many industries:

  • Alloys: Nickel is used to make stainless steel, superalloys for jet engines, and alloys for chemical processing equipment.
  • Batteries: Nickel is used in rechargeable batteries, such as nickel-metal hydride (NiMH) and nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries.
  • Plating: Nickel is used for electroplating to protect against corrosion and improve appearance.
  • Catalysts: Nickel is a key component in hydrogenation processes, used in the food and petrochemical industries.

Did You Know?

Nickel was discovered in the 18th century but was used as a valuable metal for coinage and alloys only in the 19th century. Its name comes from the German word "Kupfernickel," which means "false copper."

Isotopes of Nickel

Nickel has five stable isotopes, with Nickel-58 being the most abundant. Nickel-60 is also important in nuclear reactions and processes.

Chemical Properties

Nickel reacts slowly with oxygen at room temperature but forms a stable oxide layer that protects it from further corrosion. It also forms several important compounds:

  • Nickel Sulfate (NiSO₄): Used in electroplating and battery production.
  • Nickel Carbonate (NiCO₃): Used in catalysts and as a precursor for other nickel compounds.
  • Nickel Chloride (NiCl₂): Used in the production of nickel and in the chemical industry.
