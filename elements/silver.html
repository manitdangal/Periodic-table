Silver

Silver, symbol Ag, is the 47th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 47. It is a soft, white, and lustrous metal with high conductivity, used in a wide range of applications including jewelry, coins, and electronics.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Ag
  • Atomic Number: 47
  • Atomic Mass: 107.87 amu
  • Density: 10.49 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 961.8°C
  • Boiling Point: 2162°C

Importance in the Universe

Silver is relatively abundant in the Earth's crust and is often found in ores mixed with other metals. It plays a key role in the Earth's electromagnetic properties, acting as a highly efficient conductor.

Key Applications

Silver is widely used in various fields:

  • Jewelry and Coins: Silver is used in jewelry, coins, and decorative items due to its beauty and durability.
  • Electronics: Silver is used in the production of conductors, switches, and solar panels due to its excellent electrical conductivity.
  • Photography: Silver compounds, particularly silver halides, are used in photographic films and prints.

Did You Know?

Silver has been used by humans for thousands of years for currency, jewelry, and even as an antimicrobial agent in medicine.

Isotopes of Silver

Silver has two naturally occurring isotopes, Silver-107 and Silver-109, with Silver-107 being the most stable and abundant.

Chemical Properties

Silver forms compounds such as:

  • Silver Nitrate (AgNO₃): Used in photographic processes and as a disinfectant.
  • Silver Sulfide (Ag₂S): A black compound responsible for tarnishing silver objects.
