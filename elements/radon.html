Radon

Radon, symbol Rn, is a colorless, odorless, and highly radioactive noble gas with atomic number 86. It is produced naturally as a decay product of radium and is considered a health hazard due to its radioactivity.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Rn
  • Atomic Number: 86
  • Atomic Mass: 222
  • Density: 9.73 g/L (at 0°C and 1 atm)
  • State at Room Temperature: Gas
  • Melting Point: -71 °C
  • Boiling Point: -61.7 °C

Importance in the Universe

Radon is primarily a byproduct of radium decay. It is found in trace amounts in the Earth's crust, especially in areas with high concentrations of uranium. It is often associated with soil, rocks, and groundwater.

Key Applications

  • Radon Therapy: Radon has been used historically in radon therapy for treating certain ailments, but its dangerous radioactivity limits its current use.
  • Health and Safety: Due to its health risks, radon is monitored in homes and buildings, particularly in areas with significant radium deposits.

Did You Know?

Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, after smoking, due to prolonged exposure to its radioactive decay products.

Isotopes of Radon

Radon has several isotopes, with Radon-222 being the most stable. It is the most commonly encountered isotope, produced as a decay product of radium-226.

Chemical Properties

As a noble gas, radon is chemically inert and does not readily form compounds. However, its radioactive decay products can combine with water and air to form solid particles that pose a health risk when inhaled.
