Promethium

Promethium, symbol Pm, is a rare and radioactive element with atomic number 61. It is one of the only two elements that do not have any stable isotopes. Promethium was discovered in 1945 and is named after the Greek mythological figure Prometheus.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Pm
  • Atomic Number: 61
  • Atomic Mass: 145
  • Density: 7.26 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1,042 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3,000 °C (approx)

Importance in the Universe

Promethium is rare and occurs naturally in trace amounts in uranium ores, but it is primarily produced artificially in nuclear reactors. Its primary use is in research and certain types of batteries.

Key Applications

Promethium has limited applications, including:

  • Light Sources: Promethium-147 is used in small atomic batteries that power devices such as pacemakers.
  • Research: Promethium is studied for its radioactive properties and its role in nuclear chemistry.

Did You Know?

Promethium-147 is one of the few isotopes of promethium used in commercial applications, particularly in radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs).

Isotopes of Promethium

Promethium has no stable isotopes, with Promethium-145 and Promethium-147 being the most studied. Promethium-147 is used in various applications, including powering batteries.

Chemical Properties

Promethium is highly reactive and typically forms a +3 oxidation state in its compounds. Its compounds, such as Promethium(III) chloride, are typically used in scientific applications.
