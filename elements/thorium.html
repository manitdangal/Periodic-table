Thorium

Thorium, symbol Th, is a radioactive actinide metal with atomic number 90. It is considered as a potential fuel for nuclear reactors due to its ability to breed uranium-233, a fissile material.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Th
  • Atomic Number: 90
  • Atomic Mass: 232.04
  • Density: 11.72 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1,750 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4,787 °C (estimated)

Importance in the Universe

Thorium is found in trace amounts in Earth's crust and has potential as an alternative nuclear fuel. It has been studied for use in breeder reactors that generate more fuel than they consume.

Key Applications

  • Nuclear Energy: Thorium-232 is used in experimental reactors as a fertile material to produce uranium-233, which can be used as nuclear fuel.
  • Alloys: Thorium is used in high-temperature alloys for aerospace and military applications.
  • Radiography: Thorium is used in gas mantles and in industrial radiography due to its radioactive properties.

Did You Know?

Thorium is more abundant in nature than uranium, and its use in reactors could potentially provide a safer and more sustainable nuclear energy source.

Isotopes of Thorium

The most common isotope of thorium is Thorium-232, which is fertile but not fissile. It is used to produce uranium-233 through neutron capture.

Chemical Properties

Thorium is a reactive metal, easily forming thorium oxide (ThO₂) when exposed to oxygen. It reacts with halogens and acids but is resistant to corrosion in air due to the formation of a protective oxide layer.
