Radium

Radium, symbol Ra, is a highly radioactive alkaline earth metal with atomic number 88. It was once widely used in medical treatments but is now considered dangerous due to its high radioactivity.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Ra
  • Atomic Number: 88
  • Atomic Mass: 226
  • Density: 5.5 g/cm³ (estimated)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 700 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1413 °C

Importance in the Universe

Radium is produced naturally from the decay of uranium and thorium. It has played an important historical role in the development of nuclear physics and has been used in various applications, though it is no longer widely used due to health concerns from its radioactivity.

Key Applications

  • Historical Medical Treatments: Radium was once used in radiotherapy to treat cancer, though it has since been replaced by safer options.
  • Glow-in-the-Dark Paint: Radium was used in luminous paint for clocks, watches, and aircraft instruments due to its ability to emit a glowing light.

Did You Know?

Radium's radioactive decay produces radon gas, which is a health hazard when accumulated in confined spaces.

Isotopes of Radium

Radium-226 is the most common isotope of radium and is used in scientific research. Other isotopes of radium also emit alpha particles and have various applications in the study of radioactivity.

Chemical Properties

As an alkaline earth metal, radium reacts with water to produce hydrogen gas. It also forms salts such as radium chloride and radium sulfate, which are highly radioactive and require careful handling.
