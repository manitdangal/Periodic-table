Protactinium

Protactinium, symbol Pa, is a highly radioactive element with atomic number 91. It is found in trace amounts in uranium ores and is used mainly in scientific research due to its rarity and radioactivity.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Pa
  • Atomic Number: 91
  • Atomic Mass: 231.04
  • Density: 15.37 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1,517 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4,028 °C (estimated)

Importance in the Universe

Protactinium plays a role in nuclear science, particularly in the study of nuclear fission and reactor design. It is also significant in understanding the production of uranium-233, which has potential for use in nuclear reactors.

Key Applications

  • Scientific Research: Protactinium is mainly used for research purposes, particularly in studies of nuclear fuel cycles and reactor design.
  • Nuclear Reactors: As a precursor to uranium-233, protactinium can play a role in nuclear reactors, although it is not widely used due to its rarity.

Did You Know?

Protactinium was named after the Greek word "protos," meaning "first," because it is a precursor to uranium-233 in the decay chain of uranium-238.

Isotopes of Protactinium

The most stable isotope of protactinium is Protactinium-231, which has a half-life of 32,760 years. It is produced naturally as part of the decay chain of uranium-235.

Chemical Properties

Protactinium is a dense, metallic element that readily forms compounds in the +5 oxidation state, such as Protactinium pentachloride (PaCl₅). It is highly reactive and must be handled with care due to its radioactivity.
