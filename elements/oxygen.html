Oxygen

Oxygen, symbol O, is the eighth element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 8. It is a highly reactive non-metal and a vital component of life, forming the basis of respiration for most organisms. Oxygen is colorless, odorless, and tasteless in its molecular form (O₂) and is essential for combustion processes.

General Properties

  • Symbol: O
  • Atomic Number: 8
  • Atomic Mass: 15.999 amu
  • Density: 1.429 g/L (at 0°C and 1 atm)
  • State at Room Temperature: Gas
  • Melting Point: -218.79°C
  • Boiling Point: -182.96°C

Importance in the Universe

Oxygen is the third most abundant element in the universe by mass and plays a crucial role in the formation of life-supporting compounds, such as water (H₂O). It is abundant in the Earth's crust and atmosphere and is necessary for the respiration of most organisms. In stars, oxygen plays a key role in the synthesis of heavier elements through stellar nucleosynthesis.

Key Applications

Oxygen is essential for various processes and industries:

  • Respiration: Oxygen is vital for cellular respiration in animals, plants, and many microorganisms.
  • Combustion: Oxygen is required for combustion, making it essential in fire and energy production.
  • Medical Use: Oxygen therapy is used to treat patients with respiratory problems, such as asthma and COPD.
  • Steel Production: Oxygen is used in the Bessemer process to produce steel by removing impurities from molten iron.

Did You Know?

Oxygen was first discovered as a distinct element by Joseph Priestley in 1774, although it was later named and understood by Antoine Lavoisier in the late 18th century.

Chemical Properties

Oxygen is a highly reactive element and forms compounds with most other elements. Some notable compounds include:

  • Water (H₂O): Formed when oxygen reacts with hydrogen, this is essential for life.
  • Ozone (O₃): A molecule made up of three oxygen atoms, which forms the ozone layer that protects Earth from harmful UV radiation.
  • Oxides: Oxygen forms various oxides, including carbon dioxide (CO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), which are important in environmental chemistry.
