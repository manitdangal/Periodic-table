Berkelium

Berkelium, symbol Bk, is an actinide element with atomic number 97. It was first synthesized in 1949 and is named after the city of Berkeley, California, where it was discovered. Berkelium is a radioactive element that has important applications in research and nuclear chemistry.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Bk
  • Atomic Number: 97
  • Atomic Mass: 247.07
  • Density: 14.78 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 986 °C
  • Boiling Point: Unknown (highly reactive and radioactive)

Importance in the Universe

Berkelium is important primarily for research in nuclear chemistry and physics. While it has no significant commercial uses, it helps scientists study the properties of other transuranic elements and understand nuclear fission reactions.

Key Applications

  • Scientific Research: Berkelium is used in scientific experiments, especially in the creation of heavier elements. It is also used to produce other transuranic elements like californium and einsteinium.
  • Neutron Source: It is used in the production of neutron sources for radiography and nuclear reactor applications.
  • Plutonium Production: Berkelium-249 is used to produce plutonium-250 for research into advanced nuclear fuel cycles.

Did You Know?

Berkelium was the first element to be synthesized in a particle accelerator, marking a significant milestone in the study of artificial elements.

Isotopes of Berkelium

The most stable isotope of berkelium is Bk-247, with a half-life of 1,380 years. Other isotopes have much shorter half-lives, making them useful primarily in research applications.

Chemical Properties

Berkelium exhibits a +3 oxidation state in its compounds, with chemical properties similar to those of other actinides. It reacts with oxygen to form berkelium oxide, and it can also form compounds with halogens and other nonmetals.
