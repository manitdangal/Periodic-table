Potassium

Potassium, symbol K, is the 19th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 19. It is a soft, silvery-white metal and is highly reactive, especially in water. Potassium is an alkali metal and is an essential nutrient in the human diet.

General Properties

  • Symbol: K
  • Atomic Number: 19
  • Atomic Mass: 39.098 amu
  • Density: 0.862 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 63.5°C
  • Boiling Point: 759°C

Importance in the Universe

Potassium is the seventh most abundant element in Earth's crust and is vital for all living organisms. In nature, it is typically found in compounds such as potassium chloride (KCl), which is commonly used in fertilizers. Potassium is also an important component in many minerals, including feldspar.

Key Applications

Potassium is used in various industrial and biological processes:

  • Fertilizers: Potassium is essential for plant growth and is a key component of fertilizers.
  • Salt Substitute: Potassium chloride is often used as a salt substitute for people who need to reduce their sodium intake.
  • Fireworks: Potassium compounds are used to produce purple and violet colors in fireworks.
  • Biological Role: Potassium ions are critical for nerve function and muscle contraction in the human body.

Did You Know?

Potassium was discovered in 1807 by Humphry Davy through the electrolysis of potassium hydroxide (KOH). It is named after the word "potash," which refers to potassium-containing compounds found in plant ashes.

Isotopes of Potassium

Potassium has three naturally occurring isotopes:

  • Potassium-39: The most abundant isotope, making up 93.26% of naturally occurring potassium.
  • Potassium-40: A radioactive isotope that decays into argon-40 and calcium-40. It is used in radiometric dating.
  • Potassium-41: A stable isotope with an abundance of 6.73%.

Chemical Properties

Potassium is highly reactive, especially with water, and readily forms ionic compounds. Some of its key chemical properties include:

  • Potassium Hydroxide (KOH): A strong base formed when potassium reacts with water, often used in industrial processes.
  • Potassium Salt Compounds: Potassium forms various salts, including potassium chloride (KCl), potassium nitrate (KNO₃), and potassium sulfate (K₂SO₄), which are widely used in fertilizers and chemical industries.
