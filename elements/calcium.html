Calcium

Calcium, symbol Ca, is the 20th element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 20. It is a soft, silvery-white alkaline earth metal that is essential for living organisms. Calcium is most commonly found in the Earth's crust and is crucial for biological functions.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Ca
  • Atomic Number: 20
  • Atomic Mass: 40.078 amu
  • Density: 1.54 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 842°C
  • Boiling Point: 1484°C

Importance in the Universe

Calcium is the fifth most abundant element in Earth's crust and is essential for life. It is found in a variety of minerals such as limestone, gypsum, and dolomite. In space, calcium is produced in large stars during the later stages of their evolution and is a key component of planetary bodies.

Key Applications

Calcium has several important uses both in industrial applications and biological systems:

  • Bone and Teeth Health: Calcium is a major component of bones and teeth, making it essential for maintaining bone density and strength.
  • Cement and Concrete: Calcium compounds like calcium oxide (lime) and calcium carbonate (chalk) are crucial in the production of cement and concrete.
  • Metallurgy: Calcium is used as a reducing agent in the production of metals such as thorium and uranium.
  • Food Additive: Calcium is added to certain foods and supplements to help prevent bone loss and calcium deficiencies.

Did You Know?

Calcium was first isolated in 1808 by Humphry Davy through the electrolysis of lime. It plays a critical role in muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and blood clotting in the human body.

Isotopes of Calcium

Calcium has several isotopes, the most stable and naturally occurring of which are:

  • Calcium-40: The most abundant isotope, making up about 96.9% of naturally occurring calcium.
  • Calcium-42: A stable isotope with an abundance of about 0.6%.
  • Calcium-43: Another stable isotope, with a small natural abundance of 0.135%.
  • Calcium-44: A stable isotope with an abundance of about 2.1%.

Chemical Properties

Calcium is highly reactive, particularly with water, and readily forms compounds with other elements. Some of its key chemical properties include:

  • Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃): Commonly found in rocks, shells, and pearls. It is used in construction materials and as a dietary supplement.
  • Calcium Hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂): Known as slaked lime, it is used in water treatment and as an antacid.
  • Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂): Used as a de-icer on roads and in various industrial processes.
