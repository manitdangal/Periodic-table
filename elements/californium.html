Californium

Californium, symbol Cf, is a synthetic radioactive element with atomic number 98. It was discovered in 1950 and named after the state of California. Californium has important applications in nuclear reactors and medical devices.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Cf
  • Atomic Number: 98
  • Atomic Mass: 251.08
  • Density: 15.1 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 900 °C (estimated)
  • Boiling Point: Unknown

Importance in the Universe

Californium is highly important in nuclear chemistry and physics. Its isotopes, particularly Californium-252, are used as neutron sources in nuclear reactors and for radiation therapy in medical applications.

Key Applications

  • Neutron Source: Californium-252 is used in neutron radiography, helping in the detection of defects in metal parts, as well as in oil and gas exploration.
  • Nuclear Reactors: Californium is used in certain types of nuclear reactors as a neutron initiator, which helps to start the fission process.
  • Medical Applications: Californium is used in radiation therapy for cancer treatment and for producing medical isotopes.

Did You Know?

Californium-252 is one of the most powerful neutron sources ever discovered and is used for a variety of nuclear applications, including helping to detect nuclear material in security operations.

Isotopes of Californium

The most well-known and useful isotope is Californium-252, which has a half-life of 2.645 years and is used extensively in neutron activation analysis and as a neutron source in reactors.

Chemical Properties

Californium reacts with oxygen to form Californium oxide (CfO) and reacts with halogens to form compounds like Californium chloride (CfCl₃). Its chemical properties are similar to other actinides such as berkelium and curium.
