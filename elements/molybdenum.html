Molybdenum

Molybdenum, symbol Mo, is the 42nd element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 42. It is a transition metal that plays a vital role in the production of high-strength alloys and is crucial for life processes in organisms.

General Properties

  • Symbol: Mo
  • Atomic Number: 42
  • Atomic Mass: 95.95 amu
  • Density: 10.28 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 2623°C
  • Boiling Point: 4639°C

Importance in the Universe

Molybdenum is abundant in stars and is essential for the formation of other heavier elements. It is a critical component of enzymes found in organisms on Earth.

Key Applications

Molybdenum is used in a variety of industrial and biological applications:

  • Steel Production: Molybdenum is used to create high-strength steels, especially for automotive and aerospace applications.
  • Catalysts: Molybdenum compounds are used in the petroleum industry for catalytic refining processes.
  • Enzyme Function: Molybdenum is essential for the proper functioning of certain enzymes in plants and animals.

Did You Know?

Molybdenum was discovered in 1778 by Swedish chemist Carl Wilhelm Scheele, who initially mistook it for lead.

Isotopes of Molybdenum

Molybdenum has seven naturally occurring isotopes, with Molybdenum-98 being the most abundant.

Chemical Properties

Molybdenum is highly reactive and forms compounds such as:

  • Molybdenum Dioxide (MoO₂): Used as a catalyst and in electronics.
  • Molybdenum Trioxide (MoO₃): Used in industrial applications like catalysts and in the production of steel.
