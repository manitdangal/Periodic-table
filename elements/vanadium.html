Vanadium

Vanadium, symbol V, is the 23rd element in the periodic table with an atomic number of 23. It is a transition metal known for its strength and resistance to corrosion. Vanadium is often used to strengthen steel and is important in the production of alloys and batteries.

General Properties

  • Symbol: V
  • Atomic Number: 23
  • Atomic Mass: 50.9415 amu
  • Density: 6.0 g/cm³
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1910°C
  • Boiling Point: 3380°C

Importance in the Universe

Vanadium is a relatively rare element in the Earth's crust but plays a crucial role in the formation of stars. It is found in some meteorites and is important in various cosmic processes.

Key Applications

Vanadium has several important applications in industry:

  • Steel Alloys: Vanadium is commonly used to produce high-strength steel alloys used in tools, engines, and structural components.
  • Vanadium Redox Batteries: Vanadium is used in vanadium redox flow batteries for energy storage applications.
  • Aerospace: Vanadium alloys are used in aerospace components due to their strength, lightness, and resistance to heat and corrosion.
  • Catalysis: Vanadium compounds are used as catalysts in the production of sulfuric acid and in other chemical processes.

Did You Know?

Vanadium is named after the Norse goddess of beauty, Vanadis, due to the many colorful compounds it forms, which are considered beautiful.

Isotopes of Vanadium

Vanadium has one stable isotope, Vanadium-51, which makes up nearly all naturally occurring vanadium. Other isotopes of vanadium are radioactive and have relatively short half-lives.

Chemical Properties

Vanadium is highly reactive and readily forms compounds with oxygen, sulfur, and halogens:

  • Vanadium Pentoxide (V₂O₅): A yellow oxide used in catalysts and to make vanadium alloys.
  • Vanadates: Compounds containing vanadium that are used in industrial applications.
  • Vanadium Chloride (VCl₄): Used as a catalyst in certain chemical reactions.
